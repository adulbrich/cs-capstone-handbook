Individual Submission

Purpose

  • Reflect individually on your capstone project and articulate what you learned about yourself as an engineer and a professional.
  • Translate project experience into concrete, verifiable career artifacts, starting with your resume.
  • Build the habit of turning project work into career capital, a practice you will repeat throughout your career.

What You Must Produce

Submit two files by the initial deadline, then complete a peer resume review in Canvas by the separate peer review deadline.

  1. A reflection document (PDF, 2–3 pages)
  2. Your updated resume (PDF), with the capstone project section clearly marked as new or updated
  • Reflection filename: YYYY-MM-DD-[ONID]-RetrospectiveAndCareer.pdf
  • Resume filename: YYYY-MM-DD-[ONID]-Resume.pdf
  • Example: 2026-06-08-ulbrical-RetrospectiveAndCareer.pdf

This assignment follows the team Project Retrospective. Draw on your individual contribution page from that assignment as raw material; this document is yours alone and should go deeper into your personal experience and career trajectory.

Required Report Structure

Part 1: Individual Reflection (in the reflection document)

1a. Project Reflection: Insights and Growth

Reflect on your individual experience of the capstone project. Go beyond the team narrative and focus on your journey. Include:

  • The aspect of the project you feel most ownership of and why
  • The most meaningful technical or professional skill you developed: be specific (e.g., “learned to write integration tests that gave me actual confidence in a release”, not just “improved at testing”)
  • A moment that challenged you and what you did about it
  • Something you underestimated at the start that you now understand well

1b. Contribution Evidence

Provide 2–3 concrete artifacts that represent your contributions. These should be things you are willing to discuss in a job interview. For each:

  • Link or reference to the artifact (commit, PR, document, design, deployment, etc.)
  • A 2–3 sentence caption: what it is, what problem it solved, and what it demonstrates about your skills
  • If the artifact is private or part of a larger system, include a small screenshot or quoted excerpt

1c. Career Narrative

Connect your capstone experience to your career goals. This section should be honest and forward-looking:

  • What kind of role or work are you most interested in after graduation?
  • How does your capstone work support (or contrast with) that direction?
  • What skills from this project will you actively build on in your first year of work?
  • One thing you want to do differently on your first team, grounded in what this project taught you

Part 2: Updated Resume

Update your resume to include your capstone project. The capstone section should be a highlight, not a footnote.

Project Entry Requirements

  • Project title and dates (or terms: Fall 2025–Spring 2026)
  • One-sentence project summary: what it is and for whom; include the project outcome if it strengthens the entry (e.g., “deployed to 200+ users,” “patches accepted in Linux 6.14,” “integrated into partner production system”). If the outcome is not yet at that level, omit it; your contribution bullets carry the weight.
  • 3–5 impact-oriented bullet points describing your personal contributions using action verbs and quantified outcomes where possible (e.g., “Designed and implemented a REST API serving 3 endpoints with full test coverage”)
  • Technologies and tools as a concise tag line (not a paragraph)
  • A link to your project landing page, repository, or portfolio page (if publicly available)

Resume Quality

  • The full resume should be one page (or two pages if you have substantial prior experience)
  • Consistent formatting throughout; no unexplained gaps or leftover placeholder text
  • Proofread: no typos, grammatical errors, or misformatted bullets
  • Bullet points describe accomplishments and impact, not just duties

Part 3: Peer Resume Review

After the initial submission deadline, you will be assigned a peer’s resume and career narrative to review directly in Canvas using the peer review tool. Complete your review by the peer review deadline posted in Canvas.

Use the rubric criteria as a starting point, but write your feedback as free-form comments: your peer needs specific, actionable notes, not just a score. For each area below, leave a comment that is concrete enough for your peer to act on.

  • Capstone project entry: Is the project summary one sentence and clear? Does it include the outcome if the outcome is notable? Are the contribution bullets specific and impact-oriented, or vague and duty-focused? Is there a tech stack tag line and a working link?
  • Resume readability and professionalism: Is the formatting consistent throughout? Flag any typos, unclear bullets, or formatting issues by location (e.g., “second bullet under capstone entry”), not just in general terms.
  • Career narrative: Does the narrative feel grounded in specific capstone experiences, or does it read as generic? Point to the sentence or passage that works best and the one that most needs sharpening.
  • One concrete suggestion: Identify the single change that would most improve their resume or narrative. Describe exactly what to change and how, not just what is wrong, but what a better version would look like.

Feedback should be honest, specific, and collegial. Vague praise (“looks good!”) or vague criticism (“bullets need work”) does not help your peer prepare for their job search.

Formatting Guidelines

  • Reflection document: 2–3 pages; clear headings and concise writing.
  • Evidence artifacts: include alt text and short captions for any screenshots.
  • Resume: PDF format; ATS-friendly (no tables, text boxes, or multi-column layouts that break parsing).
  • Private links: include a screenshot or excerpt so graders can evaluate the evidence.
  • Generative AI may be used for drafting. Do NOT fabricate contributions, metrics, or artifacts. All examples, links, and accomplishments must be real and verifiable.
