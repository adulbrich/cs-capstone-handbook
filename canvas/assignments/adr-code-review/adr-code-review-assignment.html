Individual Submission

Purpose

  • Practice making an explicit, defensible engineering decision using an Architecture Decision Record (ADR).
  • Demonstrate rigorous code review that checks correctness, design, tests, and security—not just style nits.
  • Show professional communication and follow-through by tying feedback to concrete changes.
  • Build a durable team artifact that documents why a choice was made and how quality improved.

Task

Deliverable, Audience and Genre:

  • ADR: Individually write an internal technical memo for your project team and future maintainers.
  • Code review: Complete and summarize a code review aimed at the PR author and maintainers; the feedback should be objective, specific, and constructive.

Allowed Resources: Your team repo and review system for gathering materials, your ADR template; project style guides; official framework/security docs, linting, test runners, static analysis, and Generative AI tools for reserach, editing, organization, and phrasing. Do not fabricate evidence (e.g., benchmarks).

Check the handbook's Design activities and ADR guide to get started. Depending on your situation and your stakeholder(s), some of activities might be more useful than others. Follow the links in the Git and GitHub Essentials guide if you need a refresher on how to collaborate in a shared codebase.

  1. Select a design choice you influenced this term (e.g., library/API selection, data model change, build/deploy strategy).
  2. Author an ADR using your team’s template that includes: context, decision drivers, at least two viable options plus the “do nothing” baseline, rationale/tradeoffs, decision, and consequences. Cite benchmarks/docs as relevant.
  3. Collect evidence (tests, measurements, docs) that informed your decision and embed it in your ADR or an appendix.
  4. Choose an active or recently merged PR from your project that you did not author.
  5. Conduct a substantive code review: check correctness, design/architecture, test impact/coverage, and security considerations. Provide actionable comments with examples or links to standards (not to your repo).
  6. Capture the review by exporting your comments and relevant snippets into a self-contained document with screenshots as needed.
  7. Demonstrate adoption/follow-through by capturing evidence of updates that resulted from your review or ADR (diffs, added tests, author replies).
  8. Assemble your submission package (see Deliverables). All evidence must be self-contained in files you upload to Canvas, no repository links required.

Deliverables and Format

Submit:

  • Your individually authored ADR (markdown or PDF, ~0.5-to-1.5-page equivalent)
    • Filename: YYYY-MM-DD-[ONID]-ADR.md/pdf (e.g., 2025-09-23-ulbrical-ADR.md)
    • Contents (see the handbook ADR guide for reference):
      • Context
      • Decision
      • Options (incl. “Do nothing”)
      • Consequences
      • References/Appendix (optional)
  • Your code review evidence (markdown or PDF)
    • Filename: YYYY-MM-DD-[ONID]-CodeReview.md/pdf (e.g., 2025-09-23-ulbrical-CodeReview.pdf)
    • Contents:
      • PR metadata (title, scope, date, authors) copied into the doc.
      • Your review comments (verbatim) with file/line context.
      • Screenshots of the review UI (as needed) and any referenced diffs/snippets.
      • Checks across correctness, design, tests, and security (as applicable), with examples and links to standards/docs (not to your repo).
      • Short narrative of what changed as a result of your ADR/review.
      • Before/after diffs (pasted snippets or screenshots), added tests, or author replies.

Evidence to include (as applicable):

  • test logs/coverage snapshots;
  • micro-benchmarks;
  • security checklist items;
  • diffs/snippets;
  • citations to official docs;
  • timeline notes.

Follow your repo’s style/lint rules; use clear, respectful language; cite standards or docs you reference.

Quality Checklist

  • ADR includes context, decision drivers, viable options (including “do nothing”), decision, tradeoffs, and consequences; evidence is embedded (snippets, screenshots, measurements) and references to standards/docs are cited.
  • Consequences are concrete and discuss impact on complexity, performance, testing, security, and operations.
  • Code review provides actionable guidance with examples/diffs and covers correctness, design/architecture, tests/coverage, and security; links point to standards/docs (not to your repo).
  • Professional, respectful tone throughout.
  • Visible follow-through: updated code/tests or a documented explanation when suggestions weren’t adopted.
  • Submission is self-contained so a grader can verify everything without accessing your repository.

See the Canvas rubric for detailed performance levels.

Reflection (optional, ~5–8 min)

  • What tradeoff became clearer only after you wrote the ADR, and what metric would you gather next time?
  • Which type of review comment most effectively led to change, and how will you use that pattern going forward?
