Team Submission

Purpose

  • Synthesize and make sense of three terms of project work as a coherent story.
  • Identify what you built, how your approach evolved, and what that means for your team as engineers.
  • Surface transferable lessons (technical, collaborative, and professional) that will carry forward into your careers.
  • Produce the shared foundation that each student will draw on for their individual Retrospective & Career assignment.

What You Must Produce

Submit one team retrospective (PDF, 3–5 pages).

  • Filename format: YYYY-MM-DD-[TeamID]-ProjectRetrospective.pdf
  • Example: 2026-06-08-CS.042-ProjectRetrospective.pdf

This assignment is a prelude to the individual Retrospective & Career assignment. This document will become the shared raw material for each student’s resume update and career reflection.

Required Report Structure

Your submission must include the following sections. Each section corresponds directly to a rubric criterion.

1. Project Arc: Key Milestones and Final Outcomes

Describe what you set out to build and what you actually delivered, including the outcome your project was expected to achieve, not just the features. Include:

  • A brief statement of the original problem and goals at the start of CS 461
  • The three or four most significant milestones across the three terms (not a sprint-by-sprint list)
  • A clear statement of what your project is today: functionality delivered, deployment state, and any known gaps between the original vision and the final product
  • An honest assessment of scope changes, pivots, or descoped features: what changed and why
  • A reflection on the outcome expectations set with your project partner: what level of outcome was agreed upon (e.g., patches accepted, deployment to production, user counts, research results), what you actually achieved, and an honest account of the gap between expectation and reality. Reference your project’s category (FOSS, Research, Consultancy, New Product/Game, or other) and the specific bar that was being aimed for.

2. Narrative Coherence: The Story of Three Terms

Write a synthesized narrative (not a status list) that explains how the project evolved. Focus on the shape of the work: where momentum was strong, where it stalled, and what drove each shift. Include:

  • Top 3 project-level successes and top 3 significant challenges
  • What patterns persisted across terms (both good and bad)
  • What changed term-to-term and what triggered those changes

3. Causal Analysis: Why Things Went the Way They Did

For your most significant successes and challenges, go beyond “what happened” to explain why. Ground your analysis in specific decisions, behaviors, or circumstances. Examples of useful causal frames:

  • A technical decision that paid off or created debt later
  • A team process that helped (or hurt) velocity over time
  • An external constraint (partner availability, scope, tooling) that shaped outcomes
  • A moment where the team adapted well (or didn’t) to new information

4. Technical Growth: Engineering and Tooling Evolution

Describe how your team’s technical approach and engineering practices evolved across the three terms. Include:

  • Key architecture, tooling, or process decisions and how they held up
  • What the team got better at technically (testing, code review, CI/CD, documentation, etc.)
  • Any technical debt accumulated, and how much of it was intentional
  • What you would do differently from a technical standpoint if starting over

5. Team Dynamics: Collaboration, Roles, and Growth

Reflect on how the team worked together across all three terms. Focus on dynamics, not just outcomes:

  • How roles evolved: did ownership shift or clarify over time?
  • Communication and coordination patterns: what worked and what created friction
  • How conflict or disagreement was handled (constructively or otherwise)
  • What the team learned about working together that it didn’t know at the start

6. Evidence Examples

Include at least two lightweight artifacts that reinforce your narrative. Evidence can be technical or team/process-focused. Examples:

  • Before/after architecture diagram or significant PR showing a pivotal technical shift
  • Sprint board snapshot showing a notable pattern (acceleration, backlog growth, scope churn)
  • Issue or PR link with 1–2 sentence caption explaining its significance
  • Screenshot of a key decision, discussion thread, or meeting artifact (legible; redact sensitive info)

If a link is private, include a small screenshot or quoted snippet so graders can evaluate it.

7. Lessons Learned: Transferable Career Insights

What would you tell your CS 461 self that would have made the biggest difference? List at least three concrete, transferable lessons you will carry into your career, your next project, or your job search. For each, include:

  • The lesson itself (stated clearly and concisely)
  • The experience that generated it (one or two sentences)
  • How you plan to apply it (in your career, next role, or next project)

Formatting Guidelines

  • 3–5 pages; use clear headings, bullets, and descriptive link text.
  • Include alt text for images; use short captions for artifact snippets explaining what each shows and why it matters.
  • No analytics dashboards or raw data exports; keep evidence lightweight and purposeful.
  • Private links: include a screenshot or quoted snippet so graders can evaluate the evidence.
  • Generative AI may be used for writing. Do NOT fabricate artifacts, evidence, or data.
