Team Submission

Purpose

  • Produce a permanent, shareable record of your project as it stands at the end of the capstone series.
  • Practice communicating technical work clearly to a mixed audience, a skill you will use throughout your career.
  • Demonstrate the outcome and impact of three terms of engineering work, not just the features.
  • Create a video artifact you can reference in job interviews and your portfolio.

What You Must Produce

Submit one team recording (8–12 minutes) along with a brief release notes document (PDF, ~1 page). The recording must include timestamps for each required segment.

  • Recording: upload to media.oregonstate.edu as unlisted and submit the link; you may also publish to YouTube, Vimeo, or another public platform in addition to the OSU media site.
  • Release notes filename: YYYY-MM-DD-[TeamID]-SpringRelease.pdf
  • Example: 2026-06-08-CS.042-SpringRelease.pdf
  • Submit the media.oregonstate.edu link and the release notes PDF together

The recording should be polished and presentable; assume it may be shared publicly as part of the course showcase.

Required Recording Structure

Your recording must include the following three segments. Mark each with a timestamp in the release notes and optionally announce it verbally or with a title card in the video.

Supporting visuals: For the Problem Statement and Outcomes segments, use slides, diagrams, or annotated screen content to support your narration. Visuals should reinforce what you say, not be read from verbatim. A blank or talking-head-only screen during these segments makes them harder to follow and does not meet expectations.

Segment 1: Problem Statement (1–2 minutes)

Open with a clear, direct answer to: “What problem are you solving?” This is the most important thing you will say. Structure it around these four questions:

  • What is the problem? State it in one or two sentences, specifically. Avoid vague framing like “many users struggle with...”; name the actual problem concretely.
  • Who experiences it, and what does it cost them? Name the user, system, or stakeholder, and describe the real consequence: wasted time, poor quality, missed opportunity, manual effort, risk.
  • Why wasn’t it already solved? What existing approaches fell short, and why? This is what makes your project worthwhile.
  • What was your team’s specific scope? Which aspect of this problem did you address, and what did you deliberately leave out?

Segment 2: Project Showcase (5–7 minutes)

Show your project in action. The format depends on your project type; choose what best represents your work.

Product, game, or tool teams: a live or recorded walkthrough of the actual product:

  • Start from a realistic user entry point, not your development environment
  • Walk through one or two complete, meaningful use cases end-to-end
  • Highlight any technically interesting or non-obvious implementation choices
  • Show error handling or edge cases if they are a meaningful part of your work
  • Briefly note the state of any features that are not fully working

Research, data, or pipeline teams: a walkthrough of your system, methodology, and findings:

  • Walk through your pipeline, workflow, or research process end-to-end
  • Show your data, model, system, or process in action (a notebook, script, visualization, or live run is fine)
  • Present your key results or findings with enough context for a non-specialist audience to understand them
  • Explain what your approach does that alternatives do not, or what question it answers that was unanswered before

Narrate clearly. Long silences (more than 5 seconds) or unexplained screen activity will lose your audience.

Segment 3: Outcomes (2–3 minutes)

Close with an honest, evidence-based assessment of what you delivered and what it achieved. This segment must include concrete numbers or verifiable status—not just a summary of what you built.

  • What was delivered: scope delivered vs. original goals; what is fully working, partially working, or descoped
  • Project outcome: report the specific, measurable outcome for your project category. Use real numbers and current status—not estimates or aspirations.
    • FOSS: number of patches submitted and accepted; which release(s) they landed in; any press mentions, regressions, or reverts
    • Research: what range of inputs your prototype handles; key quantitative findings; any publications, submissions, or novel results
    • Consultancy: deployment status (in production, actively being integrated, prototype stage); whether the project partner intends to use the system; any usage data or production metrics
    • New Product or Game: user counts (distinguish light vs. heavy users); user study details (number of participants, methodology, key findings); any press or competition results
    • Other: report against the specific outcome expectations agreed with your project partner at the start of the term
  • What you learned: the one or two most important lessons from the project (technical, professional, or both)
  • What comes next: the highest-priority next step if the project were to continue

Release Notes (separate PDF)

The ~1-page release notes document should include:

  • Project name, team ID, and date
  • Recording link (media.oregonstate.edu) with timestamps for each segment (e.g., “0:00 Problem Statement, 1:45 Showcase, 8:10 Outcomes”)
  • One-paragraph project summary
  • Features, capabilities, or findings delivered in the final release (bulleted list)
  • Project outcome: one line stating your project category and actual outcome with numbers (e.g., “FOSS: 3 patches accepted in v6.14, 1 pending review” or “New Product: 42 users, 8 user study participants”)
  • Known limitations or open issues
  • Link to repository, landing page, or project archive

Formatting Guidelines

  • Recording: 8–12 minutes; narrated clearly; legible screen content (1080p recommended).
  • Closed captions or a transcript.
  • Timestamps must appear in the release notes PDF.
  • Test your media.oregonstate.edu link in incognito mode before submitting; broken or inaccessible links will not be graded.
  • Generative AI may be used for writing or slide preparation. Do NOT fabricate demo results, research findings, metrics, or user feedback.
