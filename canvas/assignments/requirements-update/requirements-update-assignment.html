Team Submission

Purpose

  • Align your team and stakeholders on the problem you are solving and what “good” looks like.
  • Practice writing atomic, testable, unambiguous requirements (functional and non-functional).
  • Prioritize collaboratively using a transparent method to guide early engineering work.
  • Surface product and stakeholder risks early and show which requirements they shape.

What You Must Produce

Submit one requirements specification (PDF, ~2–4 pages with core content + more if required by your project partner).

  • Filename: YYYY-MM-DD-[TeamID]-Requirements.pdf
  • Example: 2026-02-15-CS.042-Requirements.pdf
  • Template: TBD

Required Report Structure

Your submission must include the following sections.

0. Change Log

Briefly record how this document has changed since the previous version. Focus on high-level edits (which sections were updated and why), not on restating every requirement.

For each revision, include:

  • Date of the update
  • Version or Tag (e.g., v1.1, “Winter Week 4 Update”)
  • Sections Changed (“Requirements”, “Product & Stakeholder Risks”, etc.)
  • What Changed (1–2 sentence summary)
  • Reason / Driver (e.g., stakeholder feedback, implementation discovery, new risk discovered)
  • Author(s) — individual names or subteam

A table format is recommended:

Date Version Sections Changed Summary of Changes Reason / Driver Author(s)
2026-02-15 v1.1 Requirements Reprioritized top requirements and marked implemented items after Winter sprint progress. Implementation findings from Sprint 4 demo. J. Doe
2026-03-01 v1.2 Product & Stakeholder Risks Added accessibility and adoption risks; refined mapping between risks and high-priority requirements. Sponsor review + updated OSU data handling guidelines. Everyone

Note: The Change Log is a brief history of document updates, not a restatement of all requirement edits. Detailed content belongs in Section 2: Requirements.

1. Problem Statement and Scope

Define the problem clearly and set scope expectations. Your system boundaries should be communicated primarily through explicit non-goals (what is out of scope) rather than a long boundary paragraph.

  • Problem statement (what problem are you solving?)
  • Primary and secondary users
  • Context of use (where/when/how the system will be used)
  • At least two measurable success criteria
  • At least one explicit non-goal (what you are intentionally not doing / what is out of scope)

Briefly note how you are doing with regard to the success criteria.

2. Requirements

Create a prioritized (i.e., ordered starting with the most important) set of requirements:

  • Use a consistent ID format (e.g., REQ-001, can be GitHub issue numbers or similar).
  • Ensure each requirement is atomic, testable, and unambiguous.
  • Represent both functional requirements and non-functional requirements (NFRs)/quality attributes.
  • For all high-priority requirements, include acceptance criteria (AC): Given/When/Then or clearly numbered AC lines.
  • Highlight key dependencies among requirements.
  • For each requirement, link the PR/issue (if applicable), and indicate if the requirement was already completed/implemented.

Atomic means each requirement describes exactly one behavior or quality attribute (no hidden “and/also” requirements), can be validated with a clear pass/fail check, and does not bundle multiple user roles, workflows, or success conditions into a single line.

3. Product and Stakeholder Risks

Focus on risks to solving the right problem and achieving adoption: misaligned scope, stakeholder availability, regulatory/data-use constraints, licensing, intellectual property, user adoption, accessibility/privacy, and related concerns.

For each risk, include:

  • Short description (what could go wrong related to solving the right problem or adoption?)
  • Impact (e.g., High / Medium / Low)
  • Likelihood (e.g., High / Medium / Low)
  • Mitigation (what you will do about it, or why you accept it)
  • Which requirement(s) this risk shapes or constrains (IDs of specific requirements that were added, changed, or prioritized because of this risk)

Your goal is to show how product and stakeholder risks shape your requirements and priorities, not to list every possible bad thing. Focus on the top risks that actually influence what you build and how you build it.

Formatting Guidelines

  • Keep the main body to ~2–4 pages.
  • Use consistent wording (“The system shall…”) or a consistent user-story format.
  • One behavior/quality per requirement; avoid conjunctions hiding multiple ideas.
  • All acceptance criteria must be clear and verifiable.
  • Include lightweight screenshots/snippets for private links.
  • Generative AI may be used for writing. Do not fabricate evidence, artifacts, or data.
