Team or Individual Submission

Purpose

  • Create a public-facing presence for your project that communicates its value to a real audience.
  • Practice product communication, a skill increasingly expected of engineers alongside technical ability.
  • Build a portfolio artifact you can link to in your resume, LinkedIn profile, and interviews.
  • Give your project partner (and future users) a clear, findable entry point to your work.

What You Must Produce

Submit a URL and a full-page screenshot of your project page. The page must be publicly accessible at submission time. If your format is a document rather than a webpage (e.g., a published paper or a PDF blog post), submit the document directly instead of a screenshot.

Choose the format that best fits your project:

  • Landing page or product page: a dedicated website or hosted page (GitHub Pages, Vercel, Netlify, itch.io, etc.)
  • Polished README: the repository’s root README, with clear structure, visuals, and a user-facing description
  • Blog post or technical article: an in-depth write-up on Medium, Dev.to, your personal site, or similar
  • Published paper or research results page: a preprint, conference paper, or results site if your project is research-oriented

Talk to your project partner; they may have a preference, or an existing page that should be updated rather than replaced.

Multi-year projects: If your project continues work from a previous capstone team, credit past contributors on the page. You may also build on or update a landing page from a previous year rather than starting from scratch.

NDA and restricted projects: If your project is under NDA, use this hierarchy:

  1. Redacted public page (preferred): Describe the project in terms your partner approves (the problem domain, your approach, and what you learned) without revealing proprietary details. Agree on what can be shared before writing.
  2. Instructor-only private link: If any public description is prohibited, submit a private or password-protected page and share access with your instructor.
  3. Document submission: If no public or private page is feasible, submit a polished PDF document to Canvas in place of a URL. Discuss this option with your instructor before the deadline.

Required Content

Your page must include the following, regardless of format.

1. Project Description and Value Proposition

  • A clear, one-sentence description of what your project is and who it’s for
  • The problem it solves and why that problem matters to your target audience
  • What makes it interesting or notable: your “so what?”

2. Key Features or Highlights

  • 3–5 specific features, findings, or capabilities, described from the audience’s perspective, not the implementation perspective
  • At least one screenshot, screen recording, figure, or demo video showing the project in action or illustrating results

3. How to Access or Try It

  • A clear call to action: a download link, “Play” button, “Try it” link, “View on GitHub,” or “Read the paper”
  • Any prerequisites or setup steps needed (brief; link to full setup docs if needed)
  • System requirements or platform limitations, if relevant

4. Team Credits

  • Names (and optionally roles) of all current team members
  • Acknowledgment of past contributors if this is a multi-year project
  • Links to each team member’s GitHub profile or personal portfolio (optional but recommended)
  • A contact or feedback channel (GitHub Issues, email, or a form)

Formatting Guidelines

  • Write for your target audience, not for your grader. Avoid jargon unless your audience is technical.
  • The page should feel like a real product or portfolio page, not a course deliverable.
  • All media (screenshots, figures, video) must be legible and purposeful; include captions or alt text.
  • Ensure the page is accessible: sufficient color contrast, descriptive link text, alt text on images.
  • Test your link before submitting. Broken links or login-gated pages will not be graded (unless you are using the instructor-only private link option).
  • Generative AI may be used for coding and writing. All claims about features and capabilities must be accurate and real.
